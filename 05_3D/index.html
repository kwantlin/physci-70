Week 5: 3D Design & Printing


This week we worked with 3D Design and Printing, extending previous experience with Fusion 360 to creating items for printing as well as learning to 3D scan and slice.

To start with the 3D modeling portion of the assignment, I've been very into the game Genshin Impact lately, and during a recent in-game event, an item called the Xiao Lantern was released. Due to the rounded contours and patterns of the structure, this would likely be quite difficult to achieve with subtractive methods, especially in a cost-effective way since much material would need to be hollowed/shaped away.



Looking at the above image, I saw that the most challenging portion of the lantern would be to make the 4 leg-like structures near the top. After searching for videos achieving this design, I saw that it could actually be quite easily achieved by creating a pattern in Fusion 360. Here's the video of my inspiration.



I also noted that my previous attempts to geometrically achieve a shell with extrusion cuts could have been avoided with the simple "Shell" command. From the videos I watched, like the one below, I liked the idea of adding more variety to the lantern body by creating various patterns on the revolving sketch. After trying a few ideas out, I got the below result.



However, though this design was simple enough for printing, as I looked at the sliced model in PrusaSlicer, I remembered my few experiences with 3D printing filament. With such thin weaving pieces, I imagined the likely result would have a lot of "spider-webbing" of excess filament, and the thin pieces might not even remain structurally sound throughout printing.



To avoid this result, I went back to Fusion, and tried to balance increasing the width of the weaving bits as well as just the thickness of the shell. This would lead to a lantern pattern with sufficient gaps for light and increase structural integrity.



Taking this final design to Prusa, I got a much better result, with still around an hour of print time.

Next, I tried several apps for scanning with my phone. I started with Qlone, since it was free to download. The process showed me how images needed to be collected. Qlone showed a dome that your camera would need to sweep through, and to improve accuracy, they ask that you first print a base sheet for reference. I found the UI on this app to be difficult, since especially for the lower-angle photos, a corner of the base sheet might disappear for a slightly larger item like this multimeter, resulting in difficulty getting clean images.



The resulting scan was also not the best, since it seemed to have a hard time judging height, as shown below. Only after I used the "Flatten" feature did it yield a reasonable result. It also cost $20 to get the model files, so I decided to look for alternatives.





Nathan suggested Trnio as another iPhone option, only costing $5, so I gave this a shot. It works using both photos and Apple's AR Kit. The UI for the AR Kit is shown below, which slowly builds a point cloud of the object. However, I found that the surroundings of the object in my setting were too easily captured, so I switched to just using images.



In photo-mode, Trnio similarly seems to want a dome-like sweep around the object. It took several tries, each time requiring tens of photos, to adapt to taking good pictures, pausing at the right moments, and moving very slowly while keeping out of the way of my light source.



My first scan of my clock revealed an interesting optical-illustion phenomenon that would occur if insufficient diversity of images are taken: the front and back of the object might become interchangeable, resulting in weird optical effects as you rotated the resulting 3D object. From looking at other scans that people did with this app, it seems to be a common issue.



Adapting to these scanning errors, I did a final set of scans using a Beanie stuffed toy, ensuring that I took images of enough angles, especially the back. Though the scan incorporated some of the surface on which my object rested, this seemed like a common result. The overall scan was quite good!





I exported the .obj file to Fusion, and then transitioned it to PrusaSlicer as an .STL file. There, I found it easiest to do modifications like removing the pieces of the surface material by performing a cut. This minimized the presence of excess material from Trnio's scan.



For 3D printing for my final project, I started by looking at previous work on modular robotics. Here's a video I found describing MIT's M-cube.



Here's another on M-TRAN 3.



The common thread between these videos - and an issue for me - is that they rely on decentralized control, which makes sense for the application. However, that means a lot of specialized hardware is necessary, as well as wiring, much of which might not be possible without soldering. Even if I were to somehow attempt wiring together a lot of pieces, it would not be simple to control as most past work relied on a combination of magnets and motor actuators.

Therefore, rather than attempting to 3D model my initial idea, I focused on scaling down. I would like to incorporate some type of TinyML component if possible, and if I want some sort of transformable structure, I know that I need something to do RL in. After looking into some options, I found that Unity's ML Agents software would work well since it allows you to customize everything from the build of your agent to constraints on actions, rewards, etc. Here's an example showing an arm that is being trained to move to target locations.



If I were to build a structure that was completely wired togethered in a closed loop, with joint connecting rigid pieces and motors at each joint (or perhaps situated at the center), you can imagine that this structure, even if laid flat for just 2D transformations, could take on a variety of shapes. If done as a wall hanging, you could imagine sending it a shape you'd like it to take on, and it would move its joints into the right orientation (and perhaps in the right order) to achieve that shape. Whether the ML inference is done on the MCU that controls it or if that's done on a separate device and final motion commands are transmitted over Wifi will need to be determined later. For now, I would need to test the feasibility of doing this all in Unity to see if the transformations and structure can be adequately simulated, as I will need to build the structure based on what I can train for.
